**Barotrauma in Downstream Fish Passage  
Metrics, Protocols & the Reproducibility of Live-Fish Studies**

*A state-of-the-art assessment of 61 barotrauma papers (38 live-fish
studies scored)  
Companion to the workbook 'Barotrauma\_metrics\_reproducibility.xlsx'*

# 1\. Purpose & scope

Barotrauma — injury from the rapid pressure drop fish experience near a
turbine runner or pump impeller — is the single most-studied injury
mechanism in downstream fish passage, yet its findings are notoriously
hard to compare across studies. This document and its companion workbook
do three things: (1) catalogue every barotrauma metric in use and how
each is calculated; (2) describe the experimental and field protocols
used to generate barotrauma data on live fish; and (3) assess, study by
study, how reproducible the live-fish evidence base actually is. We then
identify the major problems and gaps and propose concrete,
community-level next steps, including a minimum reporting standard.

Scope. We screened all 229 papers in the passage library for barotrauma
relevance (full-text density of barotrauma / decompression /
swim-bladder / nadir / pressure-change terms plus title screening) and
retained 61 barotrauma-relevant papers. Of these, 38 are live-fish
studies — 31 controlled laboratory (pressure-chamber /
simulated-passage) studies and 7 field live-fish passage studies — and
these 38 form the reproducibility assessment. The remaining 23
(CFD/exposure modeling, reviews, guidelines, and one 1928 swim-bladder
physiology paper) are catalogued for metric coverage but not scored.

# 2\. The barotrauma evidence base

| **Role**                                      | **Papers** | **What it provides**                                                  |
| --------------------------------------------- | ---------- | --------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Live-fish LAB (chamber / simulated passage)   | 31         | Controlled dose–response between pressure change and injury/mortality |
| Live-fish FIELD (real-structure passage)      | 7          | Injury/mortality of fish passed through turbines/screws/structures    |
| Modeling / exposure (CFD, Sensor Fish, tools) | 12         | Predicted or measured pressure/nadir exposure (no live fish)          |
| Reviews / perspectives                        | 8          | Synthesis of mechanisms, metrics and knowledge gaps                   |
| Guidelines / criteria                         | 2          | Threshold criteria adopted for management                             |
| Foundational physiology                       | 1          | Swim-bladder physiology (Evans 1928)                                  |

The live-fish base is dominated by one programme and one taxon: the
Pacific Northwest National Laboratory group (Brown, Carlson, Pflugrath,
Stephenson, Colotelo, Deng and colleagues) and juvenile Chinook salmon.
Australian (Boys, Pflugrath), Brazilian/Neotropical (Beirao, Kerr,
Pedron), and European (Schneider, Zitek) groups have since broadened the
species and life-stage range, but most species are represented by a
single study — a structural limit on external reproducibility (see
Section 6).

# 3\. Barotrauma metrics and how they are calculated

The workbook's 'Metrics catalogue' sheet gives the full list; the
essentials:

| **Metric**                                      | **Calculation**                                                                         | **Notes**                                                                                                                      |
| ----------------------------------------------- | --------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ |
| Ratio of Pressure Change (RPC)                  | P\_acclimation ÷ P\_nadir (absolute pressures)                                          | Dominant dose metric. DIRECTION IS INCONSISTENT — some report nadir÷acclimation (\<1, e.g. Boys' R(NE:A)). Always state which. |
| Log Ratio of Pressure (LRP)                     | ln(P\_acclimation ÷ P\_nadir)                                                           | Used in logistic mortal-injury models (Carlson 2012); log base not always stated.                                              |
| Nadir pressure                                  | Minimum absolute pressure in the exposure                                               | kPa; measured in chamber, modeled/Sensor-Fish in field.                                                                        |
| Acclimation / neutral-buoyancy pressure (depth) | Holding depth, or neutral-buoyancy test (excess-mass / swim-bladder-rupture)            | Baseline for RPC/LRP; the field's biggest assumption (Pflugrath 2012: juvenile Chinook acclimate only to \~4.6–11.6 m).        |
| Mortal-injury index                             | Binary: 'mortally injured' if any of a defined injury set present (8 in McKinstry 2007) | Injury set differs between labs.                                                                                               |
| Injury-specific dose–response                   | Logistic P(injury) vs RPC/LRP/nadir                                                     | Pflugrath 2018; Kerr 2023; Zitek 2026. Injury taxonomies differ.                                                               |
| Injury severity score                           | Count/grade of injuries from necropsy / X-ray / transparent fish                        | No common catalogue or scale across labs.                                                                                      |
| Decompression (exposure) rate                   | Pressure change per unit time / time-to-nadir                                           | Mechanistically important (Kerr 2023) but almost never reported.                                                               |

Verified threshold landmarks (see the companion 'state of the art'
report for sources): 50% swim-bladder rupture at RPC ≈ 1.75–2.2 in two
Neotropical species (Kerr 2023); a multispecies precautionary criterion
of keeping exposure pressure above \~70% of acclimation pressure (Boys
2016); eggs/larvae injured near 100% at low pressure, protected above
\~40% of surface pressure (Boys 2016); lamprey and American eel
essentially barotrauma-immune (Colotelo 2012; Pflugrath 2019); but
physostome cypriniform larvae among the most sensitive (Zitek 2026).

# 4\. Protocols used

## 4.1 Laboratory (pressure-chamber / simulated passage)

The standard design holds live fish in a hyper/hypobaric ('barotrauma')
chamber, allows them to acclimate to a chosen pressure/depth (ideally to
neutral buoyancy), then drives a programmed pressure profile from an
onset pressure down to a nadir to mimic turbine passage, after which
fish are held and assessed for injury and delayed mortality. Variants
include the PNNL Mobile Aquatic Barotrauma Laboratory (Stephenson 2010),
transparent-fish observation (Pflugrath 2020), and dedicated
multi-species chambers operating 14–450 kPa (Schneider 2025). Injury is
scored by necropsy, X-ray, or direct observation against an injury list
(swim-bladder rupture, exophthalmia, emboli, haemorrhage, organ
prolapse).

## 4.2 Field (live-fish passage)

Live fish are released through a real structure and recovered
(HI-Z/balloon-tag, netting, or telemetry), with immediate and delayed
(24–96 h) survival and injury assessed against handling/tag controls
(e.g. Normandeau shad studies; Pauwels Archimedes screw; Mueller X-ray
internal-injury assessment). Field studies cannot impose a chamber
acclimation, so pressure exposure is inferred from Sensor Fish or CFD
rather than controlled — a fundamental difference from lab work that the
reproducibility rubric accounts for.

# 5\. Reproducibility assessment

Method. Each of the 38 live-fish studies was scored against a 10-element
reporting checklist (C1–C10; see workbook 'Legend'). The score is
reported criteria ÷ applicable criteria; tiers are High (≥80%), Medium
(50–79%), Low (\<50%). For field studies, chamber-acclimation criteria
(C3, C4) are Not Applicable and excluded. This indexes whether a study
reports enough to be reproduced — not its scientific quality.

Results. Mean reporting completeness was 74% (median 80%). Tiers: 19
High, 16 Medium, 3 Low. Lab studies (31): 17 High / 12 Medium / 2 Low.
Field studies (7): 2 High / 4 Medium / 1 Low. The criterion-by-criterion
reporting rates expose where the gaps are:

| **Reporting element**                            | **Reported** | **Status**                              |
| ------------------------------------------------ | ------------ | --------------------------------------- |
| C8 Holding / recovery & delayed-mortality window | 100%         | Strong                                  |
| C3 Acclimation pressure / depth (lab)            | 90%          | Strong                                  |
| C1 Species + life stage + body size              | 87%          | Strong                                  |
| C7 Injury-scoring protocol                       | 84%          | Adequate (but methods not standardized) |
| C5 Exposure pressure / nadir quantified          | 82%          | Adequate                                |
| C9 Controls (handling / tag / sham)              | 82%          | Adequate                                |
| C10 Statistical / dose–response model            | 68%          | Gap                                     |
| C2 Sample size (n) per treatment                 | 63%          | Gap                                     |
| C6 Pressure-change metric explicitly defined     | 42%          | Major gap                               |
| C4 Acclimation duration                          | 39%          | Major gap                               |

The lowest-reported elements — an explicitly defined pressure-change
metric (42%) and acclimation duration (39%) — are precisely the ones
that determine whether a barotrauma dose can be compared between studies
at all.

# 6\. Major problems & gaps

1.  Inconsistent and often-undefined dose metric. Fewer than half of
    live-fish studies explicitly define their pressure-change metric,
    and those that do disagree on its direction (acclimation÷nadir \>1
    vs nadir÷acclimation \<1). This alone prevents safe pooling of
    thresholds across studies.

2.  The acclimation assumption is under-specified and physically
    questionable. Acclimation duration is reported only 39% of the time,
    and the premise that fish are neutrally buoyant at a known depth at
    the intake is rarely tested against field reality — yet every
    RPC/LRP value depends on it.

3.  Decompression rate is almost never reported. Most chambers report
    only onset and nadir pressures, not the rate of change, even though
    dynamic models (Kerr 2023) show rate governs swim-bladder behaviour
    at low nadir — a mechanistic blind spot.

4.  No standardized injury taxonomy or severity scale. Injuries are
    scored against ad-hoc lists by necropsy, X-ray, or observation, so
    'mortal injury' and severity are not comparable between labs.

5.  Sample sizes and statistics are inconsistently reported (n in 63%,
    model in 68%), limiting power assessment and re-analysis.

6.  Severe taxonomic and life-stage bias. The base is dominated by
    juvenile Chinook salmon from one laboratory; most other species/life
    stages rest on a single study, so findings are rarely independently
    replicated (weak external reproducibility). Eggs/larvae and
    non-salmonids remain thin.

7.  Lab–field disconnect. Lab dose–response assumes controlled
    acclimation and single-mechanism exposure; field passage integrates
    all mechanisms with uncontrolled, modeled exposure. Few studies
    bridge the two with matched conditions.

8.  Immediate vs delayed mortality and tag/handling effects are handled
    unevenly, and tag burden is known to inflate barotrauma mortality
    (Carlson 2012).

# 7\. Recommendations & next steps for the community

## 7.1 Adopt a minimum reporting standard

Every live-fish barotrauma study should report, at minimum, the
following — a checklist journals and funders could require:

| **\#** | **Required element**                                                                          | **Why**                                                       |
| ------ | --------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------- |
| 1      | Species, life stage, and body size (length & mass, with ranges)                               | Susceptibility is species- and stage-specific                 |
| 2      | Acclimation pressure/depth AND duration, and how neutral buoyancy was verified                | RPC is meaningless without it; duration affects equilibration |
| 3      | Full pressure–time profile: onset, nadir, AND decompression rate                              | Rate governs dynamic barotrauma; enables exact replication    |
| 4      | Pressure-change metric with explicit formula and direction (define RPC as P\_acclim/P\_nadir) | Ends the direction ambiguity                                  |
| 5      | Standard injury taxonomy + scoring method (necropsy/X-ray/observation) and observer blinding  | Comparable injury data                                        |
| 6      | Sample size per treatment and the full treatment matrix                                       | Power and re-analysis                                         |
| 7      | Holding/recovery conditions and delayed-mortality window (e.g. 48–96 h)                       | Separates immediate from latent mortality                     |
| 8      | Controls for handling/tagging/sham, with control mortality                                    | Removes confounds; tags inflate mortality                     |
| 9      | Statistical model and the dose–response form                                                  | Reproducible inference                                        |
| 10     | Open data and code (raw injury records + pressure traces)                                     | True reproducibility                                          |

## 7.2 Standardize the core conventions

  - Define RPC consistently as P\_acclimation / P\_nadir (\>1) across
    the field, and always pair thresholds with species, life stage and
    acclimation depth.

  - Agree a common barotrauma injury catalogue and severity scale (build
    on McKinstry's mortal-injury concept, Brown/Carlson injuries, and
    X-ray internal scoring from Mueller 2020).

  - Always report decompression rate, not just endpoints, so static
    (Boyle) and dynamic (Rayleigh–Plesset) models can both be tested.

## 7.3 Close the evidence gaps

  - Prioritize under-studied taxa and life stages: eggs/larvae,
    non-salmonid physostomes (the Zitek 2026 result shows they can be
    highly sensitive), benthic and tropical species.

  - Run cross-laboratory ring tests (the same species and pressure
    profile in multiple chambers) to measure between-lab reproducibility
    directly — currently unknown.

  - Pair lab dose–response with matched field passage validation, and
    couple both to standardized CFD exposure (BioPA / bio-hill) so
    predictions are testable.

  - Resolve the Boyle's-law vs dynamic-decompression question with
    rate-varying experiments.

*Method note: the 61-paper set and per-criterion scores were generated
by full-text screening and automated detection of reporting elements,
then reviewed; 37 quantitative claims are independently verified in the
companion state-of-the-art report and extraction spreadsheet.
Reproducibility tiers index reporting completeness, not scientific
quality, and Low/Medium ratings should prompt a look at the paper rather
than serve as a verdict (text-extraction gaps can miss reported items,
especially in older or non-English papers).*
