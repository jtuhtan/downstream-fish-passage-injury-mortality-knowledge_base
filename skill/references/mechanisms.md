# Injury-mechanism taxonomy (downstream fish passage)

Injury and mortality during passage are driven by a small set of physical
mechanisms. A single passage route usually combines several. Classify each
study by the mechanism(s) it actually studies, not every mechanism its
introduction lists.

## 1. Blade strike (mechanical strike / collision)
- **Cause:** direct impact of a runner blade, vane, or structure on the fish;
  also leading-edge contact and grinding at gaps.
- **Drivers:** blade speed, blade thickness/leading-edge shape, fish length,
  number of blades, fish orientation, strike location.
- **Evidence in a paper:** strike-probability or strike-velocity models;
  blade-strike apparatus tests; bruising, lacerations, severed bodies, internal
  haemorrhage; "leading edge" design studies.
- **Recognize it by:** "blade strike", "leading edge", "impact", "strike
  probability/velocity", biomimetic/strike-rig experiments.

## 2. Barotrauma (pressure change / rapid decompression)
- **Cause:** rapid pressure drop (especially the nadir near the runner) over-
  expands swim-bladder gas; emboli, organ rupture, exophthalmia, swim-bladder
  rupture, hemorrhage. Physostomes (open bladder) vs physoclists (closed)
  differ markedly.
- **Drivers:** acclimation (neutral-buoyancy) depth, ratio of pressure change,
  pressure exposure history, species bladder type, life stage.
- **Evidence:** hyper/hypobaric ("barotrauma") chamber dose–response; nadir
  pressure or ratio-of-pressure-change metrics; transparent-fish observation;
  X-ray/necropsy of internal injuries.
- **Recognize it by:** "barotrauma", "decompression", "swim bladder", "nadir
  pressure", "RPC/LRP", "Boyle's law".

## 3. Fluid shear (shear stress / strain rate)
- **Cause:** steep velocity gradients (shear layers, jets, draft-tube,
  spillway plunge) tear at body surface, eyes, opercula, gills.
- **Drivers:** strain rate (s⁻¹), exposure, fish size and orientation.
- **Evidence:** submerged-jet / shear-flume exposure with graded strain rates;
  injury indexed to strain rate; eye/operculum/gill damage.
- **Recognize it by:** "shear", "strain rate", "turbulent jet", "fluid shear".

## 4. Cavitation
- **Cause:** vapor bubbles forming and collapsing at low local pressure;
  collapse shock damages tissue and the structure itself.
- **Evidence:** mostly turbine-engineering studies (detection, avoidance);
  rarely direct fish injury, but relevant to passage conditions.
- **Recognize it by:** "cavitation".

## 5. Turbulence
- **Cause:** chaotic flow disorients fish, increases strike/shear exposure,
  impairs escape; can directly injure at high intensity.
- **Evidence:** flume behavior studies; turbulence-intensity metrics; often
  discussed alongside shear.
- **Recognize it by:** "turbulence", "turbulent kinetic energy", "eddy".

## 6. Grinding / abrasion / pinch
- **Cause:** fish drawn through narrow gaps (runner–hub, runner–discharge ring,
  screw–trough) and crushed or abraded.
- **Recognize it by:** "gap", "grinding", "pinch", "abrasion", Archimedean-screw
  contact studies.

## 7. Gas supersaturation / gas-bubble trauma (GBT)
- **Cause:** total dissolved gas (TDG) supersaturation (e.g. spillway plunge)
  causes gas-bubble disease; distinct from barotrauma though both involve gas.
- **Recognize it by:** "supersaturation", "total dissolved gas / TDG", "gas
  bubble disease/trauma".

## 8. Entrainment / impingement / screening
- **Cause:** fish drawn toward intakes (entrainment) or pinned on screens/racks
  (impingement); the exposure pathway that precedes the injuries above.
- **Recognize it by:** "entrainment", "impingement", "trash rack", "fish
  screen", "bypass".

## Note on combined exposure
Realistic passage = sequential/overlapping exposure (e.g. shear + barotrauma +
strike). Many "biological performance" and CFD studies combine mechanisms into
a single survival estimate; record all mechanisms modeled, and note when a
result is a combined estimate rather than mechanism-specific.
